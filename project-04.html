Key Insights
Problem
Classic over-order / under-order cycle: excess inventory holding cost eating into margins, while simultaneous stockouts caused production stoppages. Neither pure over-stocking nor under-stocking was sustainable. Decision-making was ad-hoc and intuition-based.
Analysis
Collected annual demand (D), ordering cost (S), and holding cost (H). Plotted total cost curve — sum of ordering cost (decreasing in Q) and holding cost (increasing in Q) — against order quantity to find the global minimum, the EOQ inflection point.
Solution
Q* = √(2DS/H) — optimal order quantity minimising total annual inventory cost. ROP = (Average daily demand × Lead time) + Safety Stock. SS = Z × σ_d × √L, calibrated to desired service level.
Impact
18% estimated reduction in annual holding cost. Stockout frequency effectively eliminated by science-based reorder level and safety stock buffer. Total cost curve demonstrates optimality — any deviation from Q* increases total cost.

1
At Q*, ordering cost = holding cost. This is the mathematical proof of EOQ optimality — the two cost functions are equal at the minimum of the total cost curve. Any intuition-based quantity that deviates from this equilibrium necessarily increases total cost.
2
The EOQ is robust to estimation error. Because the total cost curve is relatively flat near Q*, a 20% error in demand estimation produces only ~2% error in total cost. This makes the model practically useful even when historical data is imperfect.
3
Safety stock is driven by variability, not volume. A high-volume item with stable demand may need less safety stock than a lower-volume item with erratic demand. The Z × σ_d × √L formula captures this correctly — intuition-based ordering typically gets this backwards.
4
Lead time reduction compounds the benefit. Halving lead time L reduces both the ROP (less time at risk) and the safety stock requirement (SS ∝ √L). Supplier negotiations for faster delivery have direct, calculable inventory cost benefits.
