Problem
Long lead times despite sufficient machine capacity — non-value-adding activities were inflating cycle time. WIP piling up between stations creating a false sense of productivity. Late deliveries and customer complaints despite floor being "busy."
Analysis
Value stream map drawn for entire production flow — separating value-adding time from non-value-adding. Top wastes: Waiting (40%) — upstream stations starving downstream. Overproduction (25%) — WIP buildup. Defects (20%) — costly late-stage rework loop.
Solution
Introduce Kanban pull system to replace push scheduling — production authorised only when downstream signals demand. Set WIP caps between each station pair. Add inline quality inspection at process mid-point to catch defects before expensive rework stage.
Impact
Lead time reduced by 22%. WIP inventory dropped by 30%, freeing working capital. Rework rate reduced significantly by shifting defect detection upstream. Pull system stabilised flow and made problems visible immediately rather than hiding them in WIP buffers.

1
Pull eliminates overproduction at source. The fundamental shift: under push, stations produce to schedule regardless of downstream readiness. Under pull, nothing is produced without explicit downstream authorisation. WIP cap enforces this discipline mechanically.
2
WIP cap makes problems visible. Under push, large WIP buffers hide process problems — a slow station simply builds a pile. Under Kanban, when a station hits its WIP cap, it stops. This surfaces the constraint immediately for team attention and root-cause resolution.
3
Inline QC shifts defect cost dramatically. A defect caught at mid-process costs ~30% of the rework cost of the same defect caught at the end. Moving inspection upstream is one of the highest-ROI quality investments available on any shop floor.
