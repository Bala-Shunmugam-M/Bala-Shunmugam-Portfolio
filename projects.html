Performance Analysis & Electrical Systems of a Gas Turbine Power Station

Comprehensive in-plant training at Valuthur Gas Turbine Power Station (VGTPS), Tamil Nadu. Key Findings
Context
VGTPS is an open-cycle gas turbine (OCGT) peaking station operated by TANGEDCO. Located in coastal Tamil Nadu, it faces high ambient temperatures that directly derate output — the core performance challenge studied during training.
Thermodynamic Study
Analysed the Brayton cycle parameters: pressure ratio (10:1–18:1) and firing temperature (980–1350°C). Studied the efficiency-vs-specific-output design map from GE performance data to understand cycle optimisation trade-offs for simple vs combined-cycle modes.
Degradation Analysis
Identified two classes of degradation: recoverable (compressor blade fouling — corrected by water washing) and non-recoverable (blade tip clearance growth — only fixed at major overhaul every 24,000 EOH). Cumulative output loss: 2–6% without part replacement.
Improvement Opportunity
Inlet evaporative cooling to recover 4–8% summer output lost to ambient derating. Steam/water injection for NOx abatement and simultaneous 5% mass-flow power boost. Online compressor washing protocol to recover 1–3% continuously.

Internship · Agni Steels Bottleneck Analysis 03 / 06

Production Bottleneck Analysis in Steel Manufacturing

Identified and quantified a recurring delay at the cooler stage in a 5-step steel production line (Conveyor → Bunker → Kiln → Cooler → ESP). Cooler downtime accounted for 46% of total daily downtime despite being one of five stages — a clear Pareto concentration warranting targeted intervention.

12–15% throughput gain estimated
1.1 hrs/day cooler downtime
46% of total downtime at one stage
5-stage process fully mapped
Process Mapping Pareto Analysis Root Cause Analysis Downtime Logging
P → A → S → I
Downtime Data
Problem
Production delays observed at cooler stage — heat retention causing material buildup. Downstream ESP stage sat idle for ~45 min/day waiting on cooler throughput. Daily output: 487 units vs 500-unit target.
Analysis
Process mapped all 5 stages; logged downtime per stage over 4 weeks. Cooler averaged 1.1 hrs/day downtime — 2.4× the next-worst stage (Kiln at 0.5 hrs). Insufficient airflow and heat dissipation capacity confirmed as root causes.
Solution
Add secondary cooling system to increase dissipation capacity. Improve airflow routing to eliminate dead zones causing heat buildup. Schedule preventive maintenance before peak production windows to prevent accumulation.
Impact
Estimated 12–15% cycle time reduction. Downstream ESP idle time reduced by ~45 min/day. Overall production efficiency improvement from 83% → 90% target. Cooler maintenance interval formalised.

MBA · Operations Inventory Optimisation 04 / 06

Inventory Optimisation Using the EOQ Model

Applied the Economic Order Quantity model to a raw material procurement scenario to resolve a dual-failure mode: excess holding cost eroding margins while simultaneous stockouts were stopping production. Delivered optimal reorder parameters and built a live calculator tool.

18% holding cost reduction
EOQ formula applied
~0 stockout incidents post-intervention
EOQ Model Safety Stock Calculation Total Cost Curve Analysis Reorder Point
P → A → S → I
Problem
Classic over-order / under-order cycle: excess inventory holding cost eating into margins, while simultaneous stockouts caused production stoppages. Neither pure over-stocking nor under-stocking was sustainable.
Analysis
Collected annual demand (D), ordering cost (S), and holding cost (H). Plotted total cost curve against order quantity to find the global minimum — the EOQ inflection point.
Solution
Q* = √(2DS/H) — optimal order quantity. Reorder point set at: ROP = lead-time demand + safety stock buffer calculated from demand variability and target service level.
Impact
18% estimated reduction in annual holding cost. Stockout frequency effectively eliminated by setting science-based reorder level and safety stock. Built live EOQ calculator (in Tools section of portfolio).
EEE Advantage Energy Audit 05 / 06

Motor Energy Efficiency Audit — Industrial Facility

Leveraged EEE knowledge to audit 8 industrial motors in a manufacturing facility. Used clamp meter and power analyser measurements to identify load mismatches — 3 motors running below 40% load, 2 motors overloaded. Proposed targeted VFD installation with a 14-month ROI.

Load Factor Analysis Clamp Meter / Power Analyser VFD Sizing ROI Calculation
P → A → S → I
Problem
High and rising energy bills in facility — suspected motor inefficiency. No prior audit had been conducted. Motors were sized based on peak load assumptions that didn't reflect actual running conditions.
Analysis
Measured load factor per motor. Found 3 motors running below 40% load — a severe inefficiency zone where fixed iron losses dominate. 2 motors overloaded, running hot and risking premature failure.
Solution
Install Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs) on the 3 underloaded motors to match speed to actual demand. Replace the 2 overloaded motors with correctly sized units to eliminate thermal stress and failure risk.
Impact
Projected ~20% reduction in facility electricity consumption. VFD installation cost recovered in 14 months based on current energy tariff rates. Motors also run cooler, extending service life.
Lean Manufacturing Waste Elimination 06 / 06

Lean Manufacturing Waste Assessment — Shop Floor

Applied the Lean 7-waste framework and Value Stream Mapping to a shop floor. Identified Waiting (40% of delay), Overproduction, and Defects as the top-3 wastes. Designed a pull-system intervention that reduced lead time by 22% and WIP inventory by 30%.

22% lead time reduction
30% WIP inventory drop

VSM created
Value Stream Mapping Lean 7 Wastes Kanban / Pull System Inline QC Design
P → A → S → I
Waste Breakdown
Problem
Long lead times despite sufficient capacity — non-value-adding activities were inflating cycle time without adding customer value. WIP piling up between stations creating a false sense of activity.
Analysis
Value stream map drawn for entire production flow. Top wastes identified: Waiting — 40% of delay (upstream stations starving downstream), Overproduction (WIP buildup), Defects (costly late-stage rework loop).
Solution
Introduce Kanban pull system to replace push scheduling. Set WIP caps between each station pair. Add inline quality inspection at process mid-point to catch defects before they reach the expensive rework stage.
Impact
Lead time reduced by 22%. WIP inventory dropped by 30% freeing working capital. Rework rate reduced significantly by shifting defect detection upstream. Pull system stabilised flow.
