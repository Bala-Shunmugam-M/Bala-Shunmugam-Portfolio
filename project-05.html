Leveraged B.E. EEE knowledge to audit 8 industrial motors in a manufacturing facility. Used clamp meter and power analyser measurements to identify load mismatches — 3 motors severely underloaded and 2 overloaded — and prescribed targeted VFD and right-sizing interventions with 14-month payback.

Key Findings
Problem
High and rising energy bills in facility — suspected motor inefficiency. No prior audit had been conducted. Motors were sized based on peak load assumptions that didn't reflect actual running conditions, creating systematic over-sizing and wasted energy.
Analysis
Measured load factor per motor using clamp meter (current) and power analyser (kW, kVAR, power factor). Found 3 motors running below 40% load — severe inefficiency zone where fixed iron losses dominate. 2 motors overloaded, running hot and risking premature winding failure.
Solution
Install Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs) on the 3 underloaded motors to match speed to actual demand — cube-law energy saving: halving speed reduces power by 87.5%. Replace the 2 overloaded motors with correctly sized units to eliminate thermal stress and failure risk.
Impact
Projected ~20% reduction in facility electricity consumption from VFD installations alone. Investment cost recovered in 14 months at current energy tariff. Motors also run cooler — winding insulation life extended significantly (Arrhenius rule: −10°C ≈ 2× insulation life).

1
The cube law makes under-loading extremely costly. Motor power consumption ∝ (speed)³ in centrifugal loads. A motor running at 60% speed uses only 21.6% of rated power. A VFD that reduces speed from 100% to 60% saves 78.4% of energy on that load — dramatic returns from modest speed reduction.
2
Power factor deteriorates badly below 40% load. At 28% load, M1's power factor dropped to 0.61 — meaning 39% of the current drawn was reactive (wasted). Poor power factor incurs additional kVAR charges from the utility and increases line losses. VFD corrects this automatically.
3
Overloaded motors are a maintenance liability. M7 and M8 running at 108–112% load elevated winding temperatures by an estimated 15–20°C above design. Arrhenius thermal degradation rule: every 10°C rise halves insulation life — these motors were burning out ~3–4× faster than designed.
4
EEE background was the differentiator. Identifying power factor trends, interpreting clamp meter readings correctly, and understanding the difference between kW, kVA, and kVAR required domain knowledge that a pure management graduate would not have — demonstrating the value of the B.E. + MBA combination.
